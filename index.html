Polynyas and the Global Overturning Circulation

In 2017 aboard the R/V Araon, we deployed a mooring with instruments to measure current velocities, temperature, salinity, and other water properties in Terra Nova Bay, Ross Sea to understand the formation and transport of High Salinity Shelf Water (HSSW). This water mass originates in polynyas, seasonal openings in the sea ice that act as "windows" into an otherwise ice-covered polar ocean. Here, sea ice is formed and blown away by strong winds, making room for more ice to form in what is called a "sea ice factory." When ice forms, it excludes brine, leaving the surface waters of the polynya highly saline. This HSSW is very dense, causing it to sink and become the primary constituent of a larger water mass known as Antarctic Bottom Water, a vital componant of the climate-regulating global ocean circulation. We successfully retrieved this mooring in 2018 and are currently working to analyze the data.


The Sea Surface Microlayer Universe

A multi-disciplinary and multi-institutional effort to increase our understanding of the significance and role of the air-sea interface (sea surface microlayer, SML) as the boundary layer controlling atmosphere-ocean interactions. The first research cruise occured in 2016 on the R/V Falkor in the Timor Sea. The next research cruise is scheduled for the end of 2019.
